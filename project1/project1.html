The popular sport that's been around for a century

Volleyball is a thrilling team sport that is played worldwide, combining elements of altheticism, strategy, and teamwork.

History/Origin

The game of volleyball was invented in 1895 by William G. Morgan, a physical education director in at YMCA. Morgan wanted an alternative sport that was less strenuous than basketball and used the ball from basketball, the net from tennis, the use of hands from handball, and the innings from baseball to create "Mintonette", the original name for "Volleyball". Because the leather basketball was too big and heavy, many issues arose when playing such as increased risk of injury and a harder to control ball. In 1900, a distinct, lighter and smaller ball (the volleyball we know today) was created, further developing volleyball's presence in the world. After many modifications to the rules and the variations of the game, we now have the fun and unique sport that is played by hundreds of millions of people today.

Today, Volleyball is considered one of the top 5 most popular sports, with over 900 million people playing the sport.

Types of Volleyball Games

These are the 3 most popular types of volleyball:

Indoor Volleyball

Played in a controlled indoor environment, typically on a wooden or synthetic floor surface. Teams consist of six players each, with specific positions like setter, libero, and hitters. Indoor volleyball is governed by strict rules regarding court dimensions, net height, and rotation.

Learn more

Beach Volleyball

Played outdoors on sand courts, often near beaches or dedicated beach volleyball venues. Teams consist of two players each. The gameplay is characterized by its dynamic nature, as players must contend with sand's shifting surface and outdoor conditions like wind.

Learn more

Grass Volleyball

Grass volleyball is similar to beach volleyball in terms of team size and gameplay, but it's played on natural or artificial grass surfaces. It shares many of the same rules and skills as beach volleyball, including the need for agility and adaptability to varying surface conditions.
